Why Flat, Sharp, and Bold: A Design Language for the Web

2026-04-02 · 8 min read · design web

There is a particular aesthetic that runs through Bungie's Marathon trilogy — a visual language born from the hard constraints of a 2.5D engine, yet so distinct that it defines the game's identity more than any plot point or mechanic. Flat textures. Sharp geometry. Bold color choices. Everything feels architectural, intentional, constructed.

This aesthetic isn't just a retro curiosity. It's a complete design philosophy that maps cleanly onto the web — onto typography, layout systems, color theory, and interaction design. And it's been hiding in plain sight.

The Three Forces

The Marathon aesthetic is defined by three forces held in tension. Flatness — surfaces that read as planes, not as simulations of material. No gradients, no soft shadows, no glossy effects. Every surface is a decision, not a render. Sharpness — the enforced orthogonal geometry of a 2.5D engine created a world of 90-degree angles, of architectural precision. No organic curves, no smooth transitions. Everything is crisp, deliberate, structural. Boldness — limited color palettes used with maximum impact. When you only have a few colors, every one of them has to carry weight.

Together, these forces produce something that feels more like architecture than like graphics. You're not looking at pictures — you're moving through a constructed space where every element justifies its presence.

The constraint forces intentionality. When you can't do realistic curves, you make geometry meaningful. When you can't do smooth gradients, you make every color choice deliberate. The result is a world that feels designed rather than simulated.

From Game Engine to CSS

Translating this into web design means understanding what each force demands:

  • Flat: Solid color backgrounds. No box-shadow blur. No gradients except where they serve a specific structural purpose. Border-radius at zero or near-zero.
  • Sharp: 1–2px solid borders as the primary visual separator. Geometric layouts. Hard grid lines. Structural clarity over decoration.
  • Bold: 2–3 accent colors on a neutral base. High contrast between surface levels. Typography that asserts itself — not timid, not apologetic.

But there's a fourth element that most discussions miss: texture. Marathon's surfaces weren't just flat colors — they were flat colors with the faint grain of a texture map, the subtle noise of a low-color-depth display, the materiality of concrete and metal. This is where dithering and noise enter the web design vocabulary.

The Noise Layer

Using SVG feTurbulence filters, we can add a barely-visible grain overlay to any surface — concrete texture on backgrounds, paper grain on content areas, CRT-like static on interactive elements. The noise is never the point. It's the feeling of material. At 3–5% opacity, you don't see it consciously — but its absence would leave the design feeling sterile, weightless, fake.

Here's what a noise-filtered code block looks like:

CSS 
/* SVG feTurbulence as a background texture */
body::before {
  content: '';
  position: fixed;
  inset: 0;
  pointer-events: none;
  opacity: 0.04;
  background-image: url("noise.svg");
  mix-blend-mode: overlay;
}

The technique is remarkably lightweight — a single SVG file, no JavaScript, zero HTTP requests when inlined as a data URI. The entire texture system adds maybe 300 bytes to the stylesheet.

The Typography of Architecture

Marathon's type choices were architectural before "architectural typography" was a trend. Bank Gothic — a geometric sans-serif with square proportions, designed in the 1930s — was used for the game's manual and UI elements. It's a font that looks like it was built from steel beams. Modula Tall, used for title displays, is tall and condensed — vertical, imposing, like a skyscraper in letterform.

For the web, the open-source Squarish Sans CT carries the Bank Gothic lineage under a free license. Paired with a monospace body font (JetBrains Mono), you get the full spectrum: architectural headers that feel carved into the wall, and precise body text that feels like printed matter.

Where This Goes

The flat/sharp/bold aesthetic isn't a "retro" style — it's a materially honest one. It rejects the fake depth of drop shadows, the artificial smoothness of heavy border-radius, the muddy midtones of "modern" palette generators. In their place, it offers something rarer: a design that you can feel.
