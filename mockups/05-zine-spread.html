Why Flat,
Sharp,
& Bold

published 2026·04·02  #design #web

There is a particular aesthetic that runs through Bungie's Marathon trilogy — a visual language born from the hard constraints of a 2.5D engine, yet so distinct that it defines the game's identity more than any plot point or mechanic. Flat textures. Sharp geometry. Bold color choices. Everything feels architectural, intentional, constructed.

This aesthetic isn't just a retro curiosity. It's a complete design philosophy that maps cleanly onto the web — onto typography, layout systems, color theory, and interaction design. And it's been hiding in plain sight, ignored by a web that chose smoothness over materiality.

The constraint forces intentionality. When you can't do realistic curves, you make geometry meaningful. Every surface is a decision, not a render.

The Three Forces

The Marathon aesthetic is defined by three forces held in tension. Flatness — surfaces that read as planes, not as simulations of material. No gradients, no soft shadows, no glossy effects. Sharpness — the enforced orthogonal geometry created a world of 90-degree angles, of architectural precision. No organic curves. Boldness — limited color palettes used with maximum impact. When you only have a few colors, every one of them has to carry weight.

Together, these forces produce something that feels more like architecture than like graphics. You're not looking at pictures — you're moving through a constructed space where every element justifies its presence or gets deleted.

From Game Engine to CSS

Translating this into web design means understanding what each force demands. Flat: solid color backgrounds, no box-shadow blur, no gradients except where they serve a specific structural purpose. Sharp: 2px solid borders as the primary visual separator, geometric layouts, structural clarity over decoration. Bold: 2–3 accent colors on a neutral base, high contrast between surface levels, typography that asserts itself.

But there's a fourth element that most discussions miss entirely: texture. Marathon's surfaces weren't just flat colors — they were flat colors with the faint grain of a texture map, the subtle noise of a low-color-depth display, the materiality of concrete and metal. This is where dithering enters the vocabulary.

The Texture Layer

Using SVG feTurbulence filters, we can add grain to any surface — concrete texture on backgrounds, paper grain on content areas, static on interactive elements. The noise is never the point. It's the feeling of material. At 5–10% opacity, you see it consciously — and that's the point. This zine spread you're reading right now has three layers of texture: paper grain, photocopy lines, and fractal noise. It should feel like something you could touch.

/* Photocopied zine texture — visible, unapologetic */
body::after {
  content: '';
  position: fixed; inset: 0;
  pointer-events: none;
  opacity: 0.12;
  background:
    repeating-linear-gradient(
      0deg, transparent, transparent 1px,
      var(--ink) 1px, var(--ink) 2px
    ),
    url("noise.svg");
  mix-blend-mode: multiply;
}

The technique is lightweight — SVG data URIs, no JavaScript, no HTTP requests. The entire texture system adds maybe 500 bytes. But the feel it adds is immeasurable. A website without texture is a website without skin.

Typography as Architecture

Marathon's type choices were architectural before "architectural typography" was a trend. Bank Gothic — a geometric sans-serif with square proportions — was used for the game's manual and UI elements. It's a font that looks like it was built from steel beams. For the web, the open-source Squarish Sans CT carries that lineage. Paired with a monospace body font, you get the full spectrum: architectural headers that feel carved into the wall, precise body text that feels like printed matter on coarse paper.

But here's the thing about type in a zine aesthetic: it shouldn't always behave. Sometimes it should break the column. Sometimes it should overlap. The goal isn't legibility above all — it's presence above all. Legibility follows from presence, not from politeness.

Where This Goes

The flat/sharp/bold aesthetic isn't a "retro" style — it's a materially honest one. It rejects the fake depth of drop shadows, the artificial smoothness of heavy border-radius, the muddy midtones of "modern" palette generators. In their place, it offers something rarer: a design that you can feel in your fingertips.

This isn't a Hugo theme. This isn't a blog template. This is a zine spread, a concrete wall, a photocopied manifesto. And every element on this page — the rotating dateline, the bleeding code blocks, the pull quote that invades your margin, the scan lines in the texture — is here because it refuses to apologize for existing.
