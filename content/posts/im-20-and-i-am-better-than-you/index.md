## The receipts

You read 19 posts and arrived at the last one. This is the one where I explain why I will be more successful than you. I am not asking for agreement. I am presenting evidence.

My team placed second in India at the Cryptonite CTF. Third out of 50-plus teams at the Superteam Solana CTF. I authored a Solana challenge called Prisoner's Dilemma that 1,299 teams attempted and rated 4.23 out of 5. I load-tested it for 500 concurrent players and 15,000 transactions before deploying anything to production. Then I cold-emailed OtterSec, Zellic, and Hex-Rays and secured all three as sponsors. I was 19 and negotiating sponsorship contracts with companies whose founders would reject my internship applications months later.

Nethermind merged my pull request into their starknet.go library. I analyzed 15,000 lines of production Go to add Universal Deployer Contract support. While most people my age were optimizing their LeetCode streak, I was reading someone else's production codebase and submitting changes to a library that protocols deploy to mainnet.

Wisdom Tree passed 38,000 downloads on PyPI and sits at 412 GitHub stars. An ncurses terminal concentration app with a pomodoro timer and YouTube music playback. I built it because every existing focus tool looked like a spreadsheet authored by someone who actively resents the user. People I will never meet use software I wrote because it is better than the alternatives. That is the only metric that has ever mattered to me. Twitter follower counts are not metrics. Download counts are metrics.

I built a web2 AI triager for a major security platform. Seven agents in a pipeline. Classification, severity, duplicate detection, summaries. It reads bug bounty submissions and judges the professional output of security researchers. It is frequently more accurate than they are. The code has been complete for two weeks and sits in a repository because a server requires provisioning and the person who provisions servers is waiting on another person who is waiting on a third. I triage submissions manually every day while the automation I built to eliminate that exact labor executes nothing.

Doomstake at EthGlobal Delhi. MaskIT, a zero-knowledge identity system. OneShotter, an LLM-powered vulnerability scanner that reduced CTF analysis time by 60 percent. Plus seven other projects that died before shipping. Forty-seven total corpses in the graveyard. I learned more from the dead ones than any single successful project ever taught me because textbooks do not segfault at 3 AM and working code hides lessons that broken code reveals involuntarily.

Eighty-four research notes on DeFi security, EVM internals, Monero, Aztec, and zero-knowledge cryptography, every one written from reading source code rather than watching YouTube summaries of things I will never read.

This is a data point about how the industry evaluates candidates. Contest performance is the signal. Everything else is noise while I build toward performing in contests and generating the signal.

## The baseline

The average software engineer ships zero side projects per year. The average security researcher enters zero CTF competitions. The average 20-year-old in technology possesses a degree they are still financing and a LeetCode streak they will permanently abandon the moment they receive an offer letter. I am not describing you specifically. I am describing the statistical median of the population you belong to.

I have shipped. I have competed. I have won. I have contributions merged into production codebases that protocols deploy to manage real value. I read Monero's C++ source for entertainment on weekends. I mapped Aztec's seven-key architecture from the whitepaper because I wanted to understand how the nullifier derivation chain achieved cross-contract unlinkability. The people you are competing against are not the people you imagine. Some of them are me.

## The gap

I am 20. I have 40 years of Compound returns ahead of me, assuming I do not do something catastrophically stupid, which I probably will, and which will probably be educational. Every year the distance between me and everyone older than me widens because the people I am competing against have mortgages and families and jobs that pay well enough to extinguish the specific hunger that drives improvement.

Comfort is the natural predator of ambition. They have made peace with that predator. I have not. I have time. I have hunger. I have a stipend that occasionally arrives late and requires escalation to collect. I have exactly the right quantity of friction to remain sharp and exactly the wrong quantity of institutional comfort to go soft.

Seventy-two Twitter followers. By every visible metric I am a nobody. The metrics lag. They will catch up because I am not stopping and stopping is the only way the metrics fail to catch up. This blog is not for you. This blog is a commitment device. Public output forces accountability. Accountability generates improvement. Improvement is the only asset that compounds across a four-decade career. I am 20 and I am not stopping. Good luck.
