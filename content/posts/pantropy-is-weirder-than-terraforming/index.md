Two strategies for colonizing other planets:

**Terraforming**: Change the planet to suit humans. Takes centuries. Requires atmosphere, water, magnetic field. May not be possible anywhere in our solar system.

**Pantropy**: Change the humans to suit the planet. Takes a few generations of genetic engineering. Works anywhere. The ethical implications are a nightmare.

James Blish coined the term in 1942. Same year "terraforming" was coined. Pantropy has been the ugly stepchild ever since.

## The Spectrum

```text
Gentle:   Genetic tweaks -> better radiation resistance, lower oxygen needs
Moderate: Full body redesign -> gills, photosynthetic skin, skeletal restructuring
Extreme:  Consciousness transfer -> put your mind in a body designed for Titan
Horror:   Forced adaptation -> "you will be modified to survive, whether you want it or not"
```

Every level has been explored in scifi. Every level raises the same question: at what point are you not human anymore?

## The Stories That Got It Right

**Clifford Simak's "Desertion" (1944)**: Humans converted into Jovian lifeforms. None return. Not because they can't. Because the alien body is ecstatically superior to the human one. They don't want to come back.

**Frederik Pohl's "Man Plus" (1976)**: A man is turned into a cyborg to survive Mars. Mechanical lungs, photo-reactive skin. The process systematically alienates him from everyone he knows. He reaches Mars and his new body finally makes sense. His humanity doesn't.

**Exurb1a's "maybe I'm the alien" (2026)**: A xenosychologist stranded on an alien ocean is duplicated by the planet itself over 300 years. Each copy is slightly different. He discovers his eyes are no longer human. The punchline: his original self was the alien all along.

---

The planet doesn't care if you survive. It's not hostile. It's just indifferent.

Pantropy asks: how much of yourself are you willing to give up to exist somewhere that wasn't made for you?

The scary answer is: all of it. And you might be happier that way.
