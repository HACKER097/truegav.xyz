Thirty hours. Three days. Six subjects. Target: forty out of fifty on a makeup exam for Advanced Numerical Methods. I didn't attend most of the lectures. I didn't do most of the homework. Here's the battle plan.

## The Strategy

Cross-reference the sample paper with the final paper and the class notes. Find the unused problem pool. The examiners rotate through the pool. Every question comes from numbered problems in the notes.

```text
Total problems: ~200
Used in previous exams: ~140
Unused pool: ~60

If past patterns hold, 90% of the exam comes from the 60 unused problems.
That's not studying. That's card counting with a degree on the line.
```

## The Schedule

```text
Day 1: PDE Formation, Non-Linear Types, Transformations (10 hours)
Day 2: Wave Equation, Heat Equation Derivation (10 hours)
Day 3: Finite Difference Methods, Calculus of Variations, Final Paper (10 hours)
Night 3: Crying (optional, but recommended)
```

Sleep is for people who started studying earlier. I didn't. That's my problem. The battle plan assumes I solve every problem pen-on-paper end-to-end without looking at solutions. Peeking doesn't count. Re-do it if you peek.

## The Predictions

| Question | Topic | Confidence |
|----------|-------|------------|
| 1A | PDE Formation | 100% |
| 1B | Non-Linear Types | 85% |
| 1C | Lagrange or Non-Linear | 75% |
| 2A | Transformations | 100% |
| 2B | Heat Derivation | 90% |
| 2C | Wave Fourier | 90% |
| 3A | Laplace FDM | 80% |

Every prediction is based on pattern matching across previous exams. The examiners are predictable. The problem pool is finite. The math doesn't change.

## The Execution

Thirty hours of work. Three days. Fifty marks on the line. The plan is solid. The execution is the variable.

I passed. I don't remember any of the material. That's not the point. The point is that three days of focused effort with a strategic plan beats twelve weeks of passive attendance. The exam tested whether I could solve problems, not whether I understood the theory. I solved the problems. Fuck the theory.
